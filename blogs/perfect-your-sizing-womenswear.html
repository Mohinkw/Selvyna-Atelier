Perfect Your Sizing: How to Grade women'swear for Fit, Reduce Returns, and Build Loyalty

Bad sizing kills brands. Returns skyrocket, reviews tank, and customers never reorder. Get grading, fit testing, and size charts right from the start to build a profitable, sustainable business.

Sizing is the #1 reason for returns in women's fashion—accounting for 30–40% of all ecommerce returns (Narvar 2025 data). A too-tight dress or saggy leggings means lost revenue, damaged reviews, and customers who never come back. But most startups treat sizing as an afterthought, copying generic size charts or trusting their manufacturer without fit testing. This guide breaks down grading systems, fit'sample processes, and how to create accurate size charts that reduce returns and build loyalty.

Why sizing matters more than ever

  • Return rates: 30–40% of online fashion returns are sizing-related. Each return costs you $8–15 in shipping and restocking.
  • Amazon penalties: High return rates tank your BSR (Best Seller Rank) and can'trigger ASIN suppression.
  • Customer lifetime value: A poor fit = one-time buyer. A perfect fit = repeat customer who orders without hesitation.
  • Review impact: "Runs small" or "weird fit" reviews scare off future buyers and hurt conversion by 15–25%.
  • Inclusive branding: Extended sizing (XS–3XL+) is now table stakes for customer acquisition and brand positioning.

Grading 101: How sizes scale up and down

Grading is the process of proportionally increasing or decreasing a base pattern (usually size M or 8) to create the full size run. Poor grading leads to sizes that fit inconsistently or look disproportionate.

Standard grade rules for women'swear

Most US brands use 2-inch grade rules between sizes, but this varies by garment type and brand positioning.

  • Bust/chest: 2" increment between sizes (34" XS → 36" S → 38" M → 40" L → 42" XL)
  • Waist: 2" increment for tops/dresses; 1.5" for bottoms
  • Hip: 2" increment for most garments; 2.5" for plus-size grading (14+)
  • Length (tops): 0.5" increment per size for hemline and sleeve
  • Inseam (pants): Usually constant across sizes (e.g., 28" for all sizes); offer separate short/regular/long options
  • Rise (pants/shorts): 0.25–0.5" increment per size to account for body proportions

Grading by garment type

  • Tops and dresses: Use bust, waist, hip, and length measurements. Pay attention to shoulder width (0.5" increments) and armhole depth.
  • Pants and shorts: Focus on waist, hip, rise, and thigh measurements. Inseam usually stays constant unless offering petite/regular/tall.
  • Leggings and activewear: Critical measurements: waist, hip, thigh, rise, and inseam. Add gusset depth for squat-proof fit.
  • Bodysuit's: Must account for torso length (bust to crotch) in addition to bust/waist/hip. Grade torso by 0.5" increments.
  • Outerwear: Grade shoulder width, sleeve length, and body length carefully—poor grading makes coats look boxy or tight.

Plus-size grading (14–24+)

Plus-size grading isn't just scaling up—body proportions change. Common adjustments:

  • Wider grade increments: 2.5–3" between sizes instead of 2".
  • Longer torso: Add 0.5–1" to front and back bodice length.
  • Fuller bust: Adjust dart placement and add cup room (C/D/DD+ grading).
  • Lower armholes: Prevent underarm tightness by deepening armholes by 0.5".
  • Wider straps: Distribute weight better; increase strap width by 0.25–0.5" per size.

Petite and tall grading

  • Petite (under 5'4"): Shorten overall garment length by 2–3", reduce sleeve length by 1–1.5", and adjust rise by 0.5". Keep bust/waist/hip proportions similar to standard.
  • Tall (5'9"+): Lengthen torso by 2–3", add 1.5–2" to sleeves, and increase inseam by 2–4". Adjust rise by 0.5–1".

Creating your base size and fit model

Your base size (usually M or size 8) is your fit'standard. All other sizes are graded from this pattern.

Step 1: Choose your fit model

  • In-person fit model: Hire a local model who matches your target customer's size and proportions. Budget $50–100/hour.
  • Founder or team member: If you match your target demographic, use yourself for initial fit testing.
  • Remote fit model: Ship samples to a fit model in your target market; conduct virtual fittings via video call.

Step 2: Develop your base pattern

Work with your manufacturer or pattern maker to create the base size. Provide:

  • Reference garment: A similar item that fit's your vision (e.g., "fit like this Lululemon tank").
  • Measurements: Bust, waist, hip, length, sleeve, inseam—whatever applies to your garment.
  • Ease allowance: How much extra room beyond body measurements? Fitted = 1–2" ease; relaxed = 4–6" ease.
  • Design details: Neckline depth, hem curve, armhole shape, pocket placement—all affect fit.

Step 3: Fit'sample testing (iterative process)

  1. Sample 1: Manufacturer produces first sample in base size. Try it on your fit model and take detailed notes:
    • Is the bust too tight/loose?
    • Does the waist hit in the right place?
    • Are the sleeves too long/short?
    • Is the rise comfortable when sitting?
    • Does the hem length feel right?
  2. Sample 2: Request adjustments (e.g., "add 1" to bust, shorten sleeves by 0.5""). Repeat fit'session.
  3. Sample 3 (approval): Once the base fit's perfectly, approve it as your "golden sample"—the standard for all future production.

Pro tip: Test samples after washing to account for shrinkage (cotton can shrink 3–5% even if pre-shrunk).

Grading your full size run

Once your base size is approved, your manufacturer grades the pattern up and down to create the full range.

Standard size run options

  • Core 5 sizes (XS–XL): Covers 80% of customers; lowest MOQ requirement.
  • Extended 7 sizes (XS–2XL): Adds inclusive sizing; moderate MOQ increase.
  • Full inclusive (XS–3XL or 4XL): Broader appeal; higher MOQ and inventory complexity.
  • Numeric sizing (0–16): More precise than S/M/L; common for contemporary brands.

Fit'sample every size (or at least 3 sizes)

don't assume grading is perfect—always test fit in at least XS, M, and XL (or end sizes). Common grading errors:

  • Armholes that are too tight in larger sizes
  • Proportions that look off in XS (e.g., pockets too big, straps too wide)
  • Rise that's uncomfortable in smaller or larger sizes
  • Waistbands that roll or dig in

Creating accurate size charts

Your size chart is a conversion tool from body measurements to garment size. A bad chart = high returns.

What to include in your size chart

  • Body measurements (recommended): Customer measures their bust, waist, and hip in inches. Chart shows which size to order.
  • Garment measurements (advanced): Actual flat-lay measurements of the finished garment. Useful for fit'savvy customers.
  • Fit guidance: "True to size," "Runs small—size up," "Relaxed fit," or "Compressive fit."
  • Model stats: "Model is 5'7", wearing size S, bust 34", waist 26", hip 36"."
  • Fabric note: "95% cotton, 5% spandex with slight stretch" or "No stretch—order true to size."

Example size chart (tops/dresses)

Size Bust (in) Waist (in) Hip (in)
XS 32–34 24–26 34–36
34–36 26–28 36–38
M 36–38 28–30 38–40
L 38–40 30–32 40–42
XL 40–42 32–34 42–44

How to display your size chart

  • Product page placement: Above the fold, next to "Add to Cart" button. Make it clickable (modal or tooltip).
  • Visual diagram: Include illustrated body diagram showing where to measure (bust at fullest point, waist at natural waist, hip at fullest part).
  • Video guide: 30-second video showing how to measure yourself correctly.
  • Size recommendation tool: "Find your size" quiz that asks for measurements and recommends a size.

Reducing returns with fit'strategies

1) Transparent photography

  • Show garments on models of varying sizes (e.g., size 4 and size 12) with stats listed.
  • Include flat-lay photos showing garment dimensions.
  • Add close-ups of fit details (waistband stretch, neckline depth, length on body).

2) Customer reviews with photos

  • Encourage buyers to leave photo reviews with their size and stats.
  • Offer 10% off next order for photo reviews.
  • Feature reviews that show fit on different body types.

3) Fit feedback loop

  • Email customers 2 weeks post-purchase: "How did [product] fit?"
  • Track feedback: If 60% say "runs small," update your size chart and adjust future patterns.
  • Add fit notes to product pages: "Based on 200+ reviews, customers say this runs true to size."

4) Virtual fit tools

  • Use apps like Bold Product Options or Fit Finder to recommend sizes based on user input.
  • AI-powered tools like Fit Analytics analyze body measurements and suggest optimal size.

Common sizing mistakes to avoid

  • Skipping fit'samples: Never approve grading without testing at least 3 sizes.
  • Using generic size charts: Every brand and fabric fit's differently—create your own chart based on actual garment measurements.
  • Ignoring fabric behavior: Stretch fabrics (spandex blends) require different sizing than wovens. Cotton shrinks; polyester doesn't.
  • Inconsistent sizing across styles: If your T-shirt fit's TTS but your dress runs small, customers lose trust.
  • No model stats: Listing "Model wears size S" without height/measurements is useless.
  • Overlooking plus-size proportions: Straight grading up without adjusting darts, torso, or armholes makes larger sizes unwearable.

How to handle sizing complaints

  • Respond quickly: Offer free exchanges for sizing issues within 30 days.
  • Update size chart: If you get consistent "runs small" feedback, revise your chart or adjust future production.
  • Offer restocking credit: Give customers 15% off if they exchange instead of return.
  • Publish fit updates: "We heard your feedback—our new batch has been adjusted for a more relaxed fit."

Advanced: offering petite, tall, and plus-size lines

If your brand wants to be truly inclusive, consider separate grading for body-type specific lines.

  • Petite line: Same size range (XS–XL) but proportioned for under 5'4". Shorter torso, sleeves, inseam.
  • Tall line: Same size range but graded for 5'9"+. Longer torso, sleeves, inseam.
  • Plus line (14–24+): Extended sizing with adjusted proportions (fuller bust, lower armholes, wider straps).

MOQ consideration: Separate lines increase inventory complexity. Start with core 5 sizes in each line, then expand based on demand.
